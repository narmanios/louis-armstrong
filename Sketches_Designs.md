# Thesis

# Louis Armstrong: The Real Ambassador

### Questions:

How did Louis Armstrong become an ambassador of American culture before he was ever officially asked by the U.S. government to serve as one?<br>
In what ways did his life and career position him as one of the first Black global stars and one of the earliest international representatives of jazz? <br>
How did his official Africa tour of 1960–1961, especially his visit to the Congo during a moment of political crisis, reveal the contradictions of American cultural diplomacy during the Cold War? <br>
How did The Real Ambassadors critique those contradictions by asking who America’s “real ambassadors” truly were? <br>
And how does Armstrong’s lasting global legacy, especially through songs like “What a Wonderful World,” show that hisl role as ambassador continues long after his lifetime?<br>

### Significance:

Louis Armstrong is widely remembered as a brilliant jazz musician, but that recognition alone does not capture the full importance of his life and work. He was one of the first Black artists to become a truly global celebrity, and his music helped shape how the United States was seen around the world. Long before he was officially sent abroad as a jazz ambassador, Armstrong was already promoting American culture internationally through performance, recording, film, and travel. His career reveals how one artist could stand at the intersection of music, race, politics, and diplomacy. This project argues that Armstrong deserves greater recognition not only as a groundbreaking musician, but as one of the most important cultural ambassadors in American history.

### Purpose:

This project seeks to bring greater attention to Louis Armstrong’s role as an ambassador by examining how his career helped carry jazz and American culture across the world. It aims to show that Armstrong was more than an entertainer. He was a central figure in the making of modern American cultural identity at home and abroad. By tracing his rise from New Orleans to international fame, his official Africa tour, his involvement in The Real Ambassadors, and the lasting afterlife of his music, the project reveals how Armstrong’s work shaped the global image of the United States while also exposing the contradictions within that image. Through a combination of archival research, historical analysis, and data visualization, this project offers a new interpretation of Louis Armstrong as a foundational cultural figure whose influence continues to resonate today.

### Table of Contents

1. Origins of an Ambassador<br>

This section begins with Armstrong’s childhood in New Orleans, his time at the Colored Waif’s Home, and his early musical training under Peter Davis. It follows his work with King Oliver and his rise as a professional musician, showing how the foundations of his role as an ambassador were built long before the State Department became involved.

2. Louis Armstrong and the Rise of Jazz as America’s Global Art Form<br>

This section explores Armstrong’s role in shaping jazz as one of the most important art forms to come out of the United States. It examines how his playing, singing, improvisation, recordings, and stage presence helped turn jazz into a global language and positioned Armstrong as one of the first artists to represent American culture internationally.

3. Armstrong Before the State Department: The Unofficial Ambassador<br>

This section focuses on Armstrong’s early international fame, world travel, films, and recordings that established him as an unofficial ambassador of jazz and American culture. It includes his overseas tours and the album Ambassador Satch, which reflected the public image he had already developed as a global representative of American music.

4. The Official Jazz Ambassador<br>

This section examines Armstrong’s State Department sponsored Africa tour of 1960–1961 and places it within the larger Cold War program later known as the Jazz Ambassadors. It also considers the other major jazz ambassadors, including Dizzy Gillespie, Benny Goodman, Dave Brubeck, and Duke Ellington, and the different parts of the world they toured in order to promote American culture abroad.

5. Africa, Egypt, and the Congo Crisis<br>

This section focuses on Armstrong’s Africa tour in its political context. It looks at his performances in Egypt, including Cairo in the aftermath of the Suez Crisis, and his stop in the Congo during the political turmoil surrounding Patrice Lumumba, Katanga, the United Nations, and the struggle over uranium and mineral wealth. It asks what it meant for Armstrong to represent the United States in places marked by decolonization, instability, and Cold War power struggles.

6. Surveillance, Contradiction, and The Real Ambassadors<br>

This section explores the contradiction at the heart of Armstrong’s role. He was celebrated abroad as a symbol of American freedom while racial inequality and state surveillance shaped life for Black Americans at home. It examines FBI records kept on Armstrong and other Black artists, and then turns to The Real Ambassadors, the Brubecks’ project with Armstrong that openly questioned the meaning of jazz diplomacy and asked who the nation’s “real ambassadors” were.

7. The Legacy of an Ambassador<br>

This section shows that Armstrong’s role as an ambassador did not end with the official Africa tour or with The Real Ambassadors. It examines how later works, especially “What a Wonderful World,” extended his image as a global voice of human connection and hope. Through rerecordings and international popularity, Armstrong’s music demonstrates that his role as ambassador continues into the present.

8. Conclusion: How Should We Remember Louis Armstrong?<br>

Louis Armstrong is remembered today through institutions, recordings, tributes, and cultural memory. Armstrong should be remembered not only as a foundational jazz artist, but as a musician whose life and work connected music, diplomacy, race, politics, and the global image of America.

This thesis argues that Louis Armstrong was not only one of the greatest musicians in American history, but also one of its most important cultural ambassadors. His life offers a way to understand how jazz moved across borders, how Black artists represented the nation under unequal conditions, and how music became both a tool of diplomacy and a lasting form of global connection.
